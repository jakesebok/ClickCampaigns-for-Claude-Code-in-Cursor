# Chapter 13: The Fishing Net

---

Imagine you're standing at the edge of the ocean, holding a fishing net.

The net has tiny holes. Small enough to catch everything—minnows, shrimp, seaweed, debris, the occasional shoe. When you cast it, it comes back full. Heavy. Impressive-looking, even. You can hold it up and say, "Look at everything I caught."

But you're starving. Because none of it is the fish you actually need.

---

This is what most fire-wielders do when they first start building.

You cast a wide net. You say yes to every opportunity. You take every client. You go to every networking event, accept every coffee meeting, respond to every DM. You build content for everyone and speak in a language designed to offend no one.

And you catch things. You do. You get clients. You get likes. You get nods of approval. You get people saying, "Oh, that sounds interesting!" You get invitations to create workshops for people you don't care about, to speak at events that don't align, to build offers that are perfectly reasonable and completely empty.

The net comes back full. And you're still starving.

Because those aren't your fish.

---

The fishing net with small holes is a metaphor for something I see in almost every coach, healer, and creator I work with in the early stages. It's the belief that *more is better*. More clients, more followers, more opportunities, more connections. Volume. Reach. Scale.

But volume isn't the problem. Alignment is.

When you cast a net with small holes, you catch everything—which means you catch mostly things you don't want. And then you spend all your energy sorting through the catch, serving clients who drain you, maintaining relationships that are transactional, and performing a version of your work that bears almost no resemblance to the fire you're actually holding.

The small holes exist because you're afraid. Afraid that if you make the holes bigger—if you get specific about who you're for and who you're *not* for—nothing will come through. You'll cast the net and it'll come back empty. And empty is terrifying when the bills are due.

I get it. That fear is real.

But here's what I've learned: a net with big holes catches fewer things. And every single one of them is the thing you actually need.

---

What does a big-holed net look like in practice?

It looks like being specific about who you serve—uncomfortably specific. Not "professionals who want to level up" but "founders who've had a spiritual awakening and can't figure out how to reconcile their inner transformation with the demands of running a company."

It looks like saying things that some people won't understand. Things that make the wrong people click away and the right people lean in. Things that sound weird to the masses and sound like home to your tribe.

It looks like rejecting clients who don't fit—even when you need the money—because you know that serving the wrong person drains the energy you need for the right one.

It looks like writing content that makes you feel exposed. Content where you're not performing your expertise but *living* it. Where the reader feels like you somehow crawled into their brain and described their exact experience.

A big-holed net looks like courage. And it feels, at first, like insanity.

---

Rejection as a filter works both ways.

When you speak your truth—your real, unvarnished, fire-laden truth—some people are going to leave. They're going to unfollow. Unsubscribe. Not respond. Choose someone else. Someone safer. Someone with a more polished brand and a less unsettling message.

Good. Those were never your people.

The ones who stay? The ones who lean in? The ones who read your post and feel their chest tighten because they've been *waiting* for someone to say the thing you just said? Those are your people. Your tribe. The ones you were always meant to serve.

And they can't find you while your net is full of minnows.

Every minnow in your net—every wrong-fit client, every shallow connection, every surface-level validation—takes up space that could be held by the real fish. Every "yes" to the wrong thing is a "no" to the right thing. Every hour spent serving someone who doesn't see your fire is an hour not spent with someone who desperately needs it.

---

I spent months casting my net with the smallest holes I could weave. Saying yes to anyone who showed interest. Building offers for whoever seemed willing to listen. Networking with people who were perfectly nice but who didn't need what I actually carried.

And I caught a lot of minnows. A lot of "that sounds interesting" and "let's definitely connect" and "I might know someone who'd be interested." A lot of activity that looked like progress but felt like treading water.

The turning point came when I started letting things fall through.

When I stopped trying to be for everyone and started being for *someone*. When I said things that I knew would alienate a portion of my audience—and said them anyway. When I described my work in language that sounded strange to most people but made a specific kind of person stop scrolling and say, "Holy shit, this is exactly what I've been looking for."

The net got lighter. The catch got smaller.

And every single fish was mine.

---

Here's the principle underneath all of this.

You cannot receive what you're meant to receive while your arms are full of what you've settled for.

The small validations. The polite interest. The surface-level friendships where people know your mask but not your fire. The clients who pay you but don't see you. The content that performs but doesn't resonate.

All of it has to fall through.

Not because it's worthless—it isn't. Those connections served a purpose. Those clients helped you learn. That season of small fish was necessary.

But the season is over now.

To catch the big fish—the clients who change your life as much as you change theirs, the work that makes you weep with gratitude, the impact that justifies every second of the journey that brought you here—you've got to let the small stuff go. Be willing to be misunderstood. Be willing to be judged. Be willing to be left out by people you never actually belonged to in the first place.

Cast a bigger net. With bigger holes.

And trust that what comes through is exactly what was always meant for you.

---

This is the last shadow we need to face before we move into Part III—the practical work of turning your fire into something the world can hold. We've confronted the refusal of the call. The money problem. The prettier prison. The lies you tell yourself. The charlatans. The voice of reason. The thermostat. And now, the temptation to settle for small fish.

You've been in the dark long enough. You know the pitfalls. You know their names.

It's time to build. Let's turn this fire into something real.
