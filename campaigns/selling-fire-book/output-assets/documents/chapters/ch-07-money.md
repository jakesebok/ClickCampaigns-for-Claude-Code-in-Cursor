# Chapter 7: The Money Problem

---

And then there's money.

The cruelest joke of all.

You've transcended the matrix, seen through the veil, touched the divine—and your landlord still wants rent. Your transformation didn't come with a business model. Simply holding fire doesn't mean you know how to pass it along, and knowing how to pass it along doesn't mean you know how to monetize it.

This is the part of the hero's journey that nobody romanticizes. In the movies, the hero slays the dragon and comes home to a parade. Nobody shows Luke Skywalker filing taxes on Tatooine. Nobody shows Frodo trying to explain his resume gap to a recruiter in the Shire.

But you? You have to eat. Your kids have to eat. The electric bill doesn't care about your spiritual awakening.

And so begins the most disorienting chapter of the whole journey—the one where you hold the most precious thing you've ever touched and try to figure out what it's worth in dollars.

---

Here's what most people don't understand about the money problem.

It's not just that you need money. Everyone needs money. The barista needs money. The accountant needs money. The guy selling roofing leads on the internet needs money. That part's universal.

What makes your version of the money problem uniquely brutal is that the thing you're trying to monetize resists monetization *by its very nature*.

Think about it.

Your fire was born in the sacred. In the deepest, most tender, most real part of your human experience. It came from pain, from loss, from breakdown, from the kind of reckoning that strips everything else away until only truth remains. And now you need to put a price tag on it.

How much does a life-altering transformation cost? What's the going rate for helping someone see their own chains for the first time? Is there a number that captures the value of someone finally—*finally*—understanding who they really are?

The answer is yes, by the way. There absolutely is. But you don't believe that yet. And that disbelief is the trap.

Because when you don't believe your fire is worth money, you do one of two things.

You either don't charge at all—giving your gift away for free until resentment eats you alive.

Or you compromise.

---

So you compromise.

You try to build a "sensible" coaching business. Performance optimization for high achievers. Leadership development for law firms. Executive coaching for CEOs who just need better work-life balance.

The words taste like ash coming out of your mouth.

But they *sound* legitimate. They sound like something you could put on a website. Something you could pitch to a company. Something that has a clear outcome, a measurable ROI, a value proposition that a rational person could evaluate and say, "Yes, I'll pay for that."

The problem is that none of it is real. Not for you.

You sit down to write workshop content about "workplace productivity" and the writer's block hits so hard you can't type a single sentence. Weeks pass. No income. No content. Just you, staring at a blank page, trying to force yourself to care about helping lawyers bill more hours.

You open a document to build a slide deck for the presentation you promised someone. You type a title. You sit there. You close the laptop. You go for a walk. You come back. You sit down again. Nothing.

It's not laziness. It's not procrastination. It's not lack of discipline.

It's rebellion.

Dan Sullivan said something that hit me like a freight train: *"Procrastination is wisdom."* Your soul is rebelling against something it has no interest in. The resistance isn't a character flaw—it's data. It's your deepest self refusing to waste energy on something that isn't aligned.

Because every time you try to dim your light—to package your fire into something "marketable"—your soul throws up roadblocks. The writing won't flow. The clients don't come. The money doesn't manifest.

Your resistance isn't a bug. It's a feature.

It's your soul's way of saying: *This isn't it. This isn't what wants to be created through you.*

---

I know this because I lived it. Intimately.

When I finally left my day job—or rather, when the universe moved me out of it—I had no income. What I *had* was credit card debt, a family to support, and a fire in my chest that I still couldn't explain to anyone.

I tried to be practical. I really did.

I told myself I'd build something sensible first—something that could pay the bills while I figured out the real thing on the side. Lead generation. Digital marketing consulting. Skills I already had. Safe. Logical.

But my heart wasn't in it. My heart was never going to be in it. And you know what happens when you build something without your heart in it? You build something that dies on the vine. Because you can't give energy to something that drains your energy to think about.

So I pivoted. Tried to find the middle ground. A coaching business that was *close* to what I wanted but practical enough to make money. Something that sounded credible at a networking event.

That didn't work either. Because "close" isn't "it." And when you've held real fire, you can feel the difference between truth and almost-truth in your bones. Almost-truth is worse than a lie, because a lie you can live with. Almost-truth haunts you.

---

Here's what nobody warns you about the money problem: it doesn't just threaten your bank account. It threatens your identity.

Because when you're broke—when the bills are piling up and the credit cards are maxing out and your partner is asking real questions about real expenses—the temptation isn't just to compromise your offer. The temptation is to compromise your *belief*.

You start to think: *Maybe the fire isn't real.*

Maybe I was wrong. Maybe it was just a phase. Maybe I'm being naive and idealistic and the adults in the room are right—I should just get a job and be grateful for a steady paycheck. Maybe this whole "calling" thing is just a story I told myself to justify not wanting to work.

The money problem becomes a belief problem. And the belief problem becomes an identity problem. And the identity problem slowly, quietly erodes the fire itself.

Here's a question I ask my clients that stops most of them cold: *In what ways would achieving your current definition of success threaten your happiness, your health, your relationships, or your sense of freedom?*

Read that again. Let it land.

Because ninety-five percent of our behavior is driven by the subconscious—and I like to imagine the subconscious as a little scientist with Einstein hair. His main goals are fun, feel-goods, and freedom. He wants to keep you safe and comfortable. Also... he's a toddler. And when the toddler scientist detects that what you're building would actually make your life *worse*—more stress, less freedom, wrong clients, wrong work—he pulls the emergency brake. He stops you from writing. He makes you procrastinate. He sabotages the launch.

Making more money won't solve a mindset of financial scarcity; you'll just want more. Getting more people to like you won't solve a belief that you aren't lovable; you'll just need more. The external fix never reaches the internal wound.

That's the real danger. Not that you'll go broke—though that's scary enough. The real danger is that the financial pressure will convince you that the fire was never real. That you'll surrender not because you failed, but because you stopped believing you had anything worth offering.

---

So let me tell you what's actually true.

The fire is real. What you hold is real. The value is real. And it is absolutely, unequivocally worth money. Worth real money. Worth *good* money.

But—and this is important—the fire in its raw form is not a product. It's not an offer. It's not something you can put on a shelf and sell.

Fire needs a container.

A candle. A lantern. A torch. Something that holds the fire in a shape that someone else can pick up and carry.

Your job isn't to sell fire. Not yet. Your job, right now, is to find the container. And the container is different from the fire. The container is the coaching program, the framework, the methodology, the experience you design. It's the thing that holds the fire in a form the marketplace can understand.

The fire itself doesn't change. The container is what makes it transferable.

We'll get to the specifics of building that container later—in Part III. But for now, I need you to understand the distinction. Because the money problem isn't a sign that your fire isn't valuable. It's a sign that you haven't found the container yet.

And that's a solvable problem. A very solvable problem.

---

There's one more thing about money that I need to say, because it's the thing I wish someone had told me when I was twenty thousand dollars in debt with a newborn and a dream.

When you create from fear and scarcity, you build defensive fortresses. You run from what you hate instead of toward what you love. Every decision becomes about survival instead of service. And the things you build from that place—the offers, the programs, the businesses—they carry that fear in them. People can feel it. They might not be able to name it, but they can feel it.

The irony of the money problem is that the more desperate you are for money, the harder it is to make it—because desperation repels the very people you're trying to serve. They came looking for fire. They can tell when you're offering them a fire sale instead.

The answer isn't to not need money. That's spiritual bypassing and it's nonsense.

The answer is to hold two truths at the same time: you need money *and* you hold fire. Both are real. Neither cancels out the other. The work is to honor both without letting either one consume you.

You deserve to be compensated for what you carry. And the world deserves to receive it.

But between you and that reality? There's a specific trap you need to see coming. Because if the money problem tempts you to compromise your fire, the next pitfall tempts you to build an entire life around that compromise.

It's the prettier prison. And it's next.
