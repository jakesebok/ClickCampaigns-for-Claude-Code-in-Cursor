# Chapter 12: Believing It Isn't Valuable Enough

---

This one's sneaky.

You've faced the charlatans. You've named the Voice of Reason. You've seen the prettier prison for what it is. You're starting to move. Starting to tell the truth. Starting to build something real.

And then something strange happens.

It starts to work.

Someone hears what you're doing and their eyes light up. A client shows up—a real one—and the session goes better than you imagined. They leave transformed. They send you a message the next day telling you that what you said changed how they see everything. Someone else refers a friend. The friend becomes a client. The fire is spreading.

And instead of feeling vindicated, you feel... fraudulent.

*That was a fluke. They're just being nice. It can't possibly be this good. Something's off. I'm going to get found out. This isn't sustainable. It's not enough. I'm not enough.*

---

This is the trial that comes *after* you start getting it right.

The earlier pitfalls—the money problem, the prison, the charlatans—those are obstacles that stop you from starting. This one is different. This one waits until you're actually succeeding and then whispers in your ear: *Yeah, but is it really that good?*

I lived this. People were telling me I was changing their lives. Clients were sending me testimonials that would make any coach weep with gratitude. My work was landing. It was real. It was measurable. I could see the change in them—in their faces, their posture, their decisions, the way they talked about themselves.

And I still didn't believe it was enough.

I'd read a testimonial and think: *They're exaggerating.* I'd finish a powerful session and think: *I got lucky.* I'd hear someone describe how I changed their life and think: *If they really knew me, they wouldn't say that.*

It's a kind of madness. You're holding evidence in your hands—hard, undeniable evidence that what you do works—and your brain is rejecting it like a counterfeit bill.

---

I think of this as the identity thermostat.

You know how a thermostat works. You set it to seventy-two degrees. If the room gets too cold, the heater kicks on. If the room gets too hot, the AC kicks on. The thermostat keeps the temperature exactly where it was set—no matter what's happening outside.

Your identity works the same way.

Maxwell Maltz wrote about this in *Psycho-Cybernetics* back in 1960. He was a plastic surgeon who noticed something strange: some patients got the surgery they wanted and their entire life transformed. Others got the exact same surgery and nothing changed. Same nose. Same chin. Completely different outcome. Why? Because the patients who didn't change were still running an old self-image. The outer world updated, but the inner blueprint stayed frozen.

Maltz realized that our subconscious mind works like a servomechanism—it follows the instructions we give it. And the instructions aren't our goals or our intentions. They're our *self-image*. The picture we hold of who we are.

Somewhere deep inside, you have a setting—a temperature you believe you deserve. A level of success, impact, and recognition that feels "right" for who you are. And when your actual life exceeds that setting—when the evidence of your value starts running hotter than your self-concept—your internal thermostat kicks on.

The AC turns on. You start cooling yourself down.

You sabotage a client relationship. You underprice your offer. You stop marketing. You procrastinate on the thing that was working. You pick a fight with a partner. You get mysteriously sick. You find reasons to be busy with things that don't matter.

Anything to bring the temperature back down to the level your identity can tolerate.

This isn't a character flaw. It's a survival mechanism. Your brain genuinely believes that exceeding your identity's set point is *dangerous*—because in your formative years, it might have been. Standing out might have gotten you punished. Succeeding might have triggered jealousy. Being seen might have meant being targeted.

So the thermostat kicks on. Not because you're broken. Because you're wired to protect a self-concept that hasn't been updated since you were twelve.

---

I had a client—Marshall—who taught me more about this dynamic than I could have learned from any textbook.

Marshall was a chiropractor. Brilliant at his craft. Deeply devoted to his patients. His hands could do things that bordered on miraculous.

But he hated owning a business.

"I love being a chiropractor," he told me. "But I hate everything about running a practice."

When we dug in, it wasn't the business he hated. It was the person the business required him to be. He had to market himself. He had to charge money. He had to be visible, be authoritative, be the face of something. And every one of those requirements crashed directly into his identity thermostat—which was set to "helpful guy who stays in the background."

His imposter syndrome wasn't about whether he was a good chiropractor. He *knew* he was. The evidence was overwhelming. His patients adored him. His outcomes were exceptional.

His imposter syndrome was about whether he was allowed to *be seen* as a good chiropractor. Whether he was allowed to charge what he was worth. Whether he was allowed to step forward and own his excellence.

When we worked through it—when he started adjusting the thermostat instead of just pushing through the resistance—something remarkable happened. The anxiety that had been eating him alive for years started to dissolve. Not because the circumstances changed, but because his internal set point moved. He stopped fighting against his own success and started letting it in.

"The business now feels more like an extension of me than something I have to go do," he told me later.

That's the shift. Not more effort. Not better strategy. A recalibrated thermostat.

---

So how do you recalibrate?

First, you have to see what the thermostat is actually set to. And that requires asking some uncomfortable questions.

*What level of success do I secretly believe I deserve?*

*What would happen if I were wildly, undeniably successful?*

*Who would be angry? Who would be jealous? Who would I lose?*

*What story am I telling myself about people who succeed at this level?*

*When did I first learn that being too much was dangerous?*

The answers won't be pretty. They usually involve old stories—childhood stories, family stories, stories so old you don't remember learning them. But they're running your thermostat. They're the invisible hand that keeps pulling you back to "reasonable" every time you try to reach for "extraordinary."

Second—and this is the part that takes practice—you have to start letting the evidence in. Actually *letting it in*. Not deflecting compliments. Not discounting testimonials. Not attributing your success to luck or timing or the client being easy.

Alex Hormozi put it simply: "You become confident by having a stack of undeniable proof that you are who you say you are." That's what evidence collection is. Not arrogance—architecture. You're building a case file that your thermostat can't dismiss.

When someone tells you that you changed their life, let it land. Feel it. Let your body take it in. This isn't ego. This is calibration. You're teaching your thermostat that this temperature is safe. That you can be this good and survive. That the world won't punish you for being powerful.

It takes time. The thermostat doesn't move in a day. But every time you let a piece of evidence in—every time you resist the urge to discount your impact—you're nudging the setting up by one degree.

And eventually, the room stays warm without you having to fight the AC.

---

Believing your fire isn't valuable enough is the last, most sophisticated trick of the old self. It's the mask's final play. After every other obstacle has been cleared, it shows up wearing the disguise of humility and says: *Sure, but let's not get carried away.*

Get carried away.

Let the evidence speak. Let the fire burn at its natural temperature. Let yourself be as good as you actually are.

The world needs your fire at full heat. Not dimmed. Not moderated. Not kept at a safe, reasonable temperature that doesn't threaten your old identity.

Full heat.
