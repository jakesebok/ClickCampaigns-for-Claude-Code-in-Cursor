# Chapter 2: The Promethean Moment

---

My story is painful and personal.

Like every story of transformation.

I grew up in the Charismatic Evangelical tradition of Mid-American Christianity. Under the surface layer of acceptance and the message of Jesus, this tradition taught a deep level of self-hatred and conditional love—a love that had to be earned. I was raised from birth to press the "unacceptable" parts of myself deep into shadow and wear the mask of "good boy," because to not do so was literally the difference between an eternity in heaven and an eternity burning in the fiery pits of hell.

But here's the thing—I didn't just grow up in a family. I grew up inside a *system*. And the system has three layers of pressure that forge the mask whether you know it or not.

First: the Neighbors. *What will the neighbors think?* The social pressure to look successful, be respectable, keep up appearances. Betray your heart to stay in the 9-to-5. Look successful. Feel empty.

Second: the Institutions. The cultural concepts of what's right, what's good, what's normal. Church. School. The media. The invisible rulebook nobody agreed to but everybody follows.

Third: God—or Natural Law—the deepest layer. The existential fear that if you step out of line, something cosmic will punish you. For me, that meant literal hellfire. For others, it might be a vaguer dread that the universe doesn't support the person you actually are.

The mask formed as a strategy to survive these pressures. Not as weakness—as intelligence. The child does what the child must to stay safe.

My father was what I'd call a present-absent experience. He was part of our lives but almost always working. And when he wasn't working, he was drinking. He was so exhausted that he really wasn't present even when he was there—and the only way I really knew he was around was the sound of him and my mother screaming at each other from the other room on a daily basis.

In his absence, I was parentified by my mother. Became something more like her partner than her son. Took on the responsibility of protecting and caring for my younger sister. I learned early—from my mother, my father, and my church—that love was something to be *survived*. That my value came from how much I could give without receiving. That I needed to take care of everyone else first.

I learned to wear the mask so well that I forgot I was wearing it.

---

I wore it for decades. Through an eleven-year relationship that started when I was twenty—one that felt like "home" in the worst way, because it replicated every pattern I'd learned about love being painful, sacrificial, and self-effacing. I wore it through a marriage, through the birth of my son, through years of performing a life that looked right on the outside while something inside me was slowly suffocating.

And then, at thirty-one, I couldn't wear it anymore.

I was in a strained marriage that consisted more of conflict and resistance than any actual desire to be there. I was drinking every single day just to avoid the pain. And that's when I saw a little glimmer of light—a barista at my favorite coffee shop with a smile that got bigger every time I walked in. I found myself thinking about her when I woke up. Before I fell asleep.

That's when I knew something was very wrong. What I showed on the outside was completely different from what I felt and thought on the inside.

If I'm brutally honest, I'd been fantasizing about life outside my marriage since shortly after I entered it. But my paradigm of love taught me that love was supposed to feel conflicted, painful, and sacrificial. To have been honest about my true desire to leave would have threatened my identity as a person who was strong, loving, and *good*.

But the dissonance had become too much to ignore. So I told my wife the truth. It wasn't a surprise to her. Of course she already knew. We both did. We just didn't want to disrupt the life we had.

So I moved out.

It was the scariest thing either of us had ever done. We told our families and at that very moment, the mask I had been wearing—the good boy, the spiritual leader, the present father—shattered. I was exposed, vulnerable, bare for all to see.

There was nothing left to lose.

So I decided to take advantage of the opportunity.

---

With more time on my hands, I began meditating, reading, and journaling. That's when I stumbled upon the work of Carl Jung and Alan Watts.

Watts gave me new concepts about the nature of reality, identity, and the world I lived in. He taught me that reality is not the labels we place upon it—it's something far more original than that. That all judgment is a linguistic construction, but reality itself is pre-linguistic.

Jung gave vocabulary to the battles I'd been struggling with. He described the Ego as the tip of an iceberg with most of its mass below the surface. He spoke of the Shadow—those aspects of ourselves we repress because they're too dangerous to own. And he spoke of the Persona: a mask we wear and come to associate with so heavily that we believe it's all there is.

Peeling back this Persona layer is often the beginning of awakening. And that was exactly what was happening. I had peeled back the mask of the good boy and allowed myself to speak the truth without fear. I was getting to know my true self for the first time.

And then I found Campbell.

Joseph Campbell. Joey Campbs, as one of my favorite comedians calls him. I'd tried to read his work before—it didn't mean anything to me yet. I wasn't ready.

But having experienced Jung's framework, when I watched a lecture series called *Mythos*, something clicked in a way that changed everything. Campbell showed a diagram illustrating Jung's concept of the psyche and described, step by step, how the myths of cultures across time and space describe not an adventure that takes the hero outward into finite space, but *inward* into the infinite nature of the self.

My own Hero's Journey was a journey back home to the truth of who I was at my core—beneath the layers of identity, judgment, and language constructed by the mind.

Watts showed me that the labels weren't real.

Jung showed me who built them and why.

Campbell showed me the map home.

I was on a quest. And I would stop at nothing.

---

During this time, something unexpected happened that would crack me open further than any book ever could.

I'd been pursuing a woman online—someone I'd met on a dating app. She was more enticing, more challenging, more irresistible than anyone I'd encountered. I pursued her like a prize. But she wasn't something to be contained. When I asked her to dinner, she said, "You're going to have to prove that you've got a little something extra first. I'll know it when I see it."

That became a challenge. A bar to meet and surpass. Though I didn't know it yet, she had just exposed a wound I was actively ripping wide open—my need to feel worthy.

We texted for months. She became a mirror. I projected all of my pain and all of my wounds onto her, healed them, and then sent the text asking if I'd proved myself yet. She impacted my life more than any other single person had in just a few months.

To this day, we have never met in person.

I once wrote her a story—a steamy, elaborate fantasy—and when she read it, she told me the person I'd described was nothing like her. I'd been seeing my projection, not her. Your longing for another is always a longing for yourself.

At some point, I realized she wasn't the person I wanted to be with. I had fantasized that she was someone else based on my own wounds. She had helped me see myself closely enough to realize something that shook the foundation of everything I'd believed:

I was never going to find wholeness in another person.

I was already whole.

She told me she already knew that. She was just proud of me for doing the work.

That was one transformation. The healing of a wound I'd carried since childhood—the belief that I wasn't enough. That's when I began walking in my true value and worth.

But there was another transformation happening at the same time. A deeper one. The one that would truly change everything.

---

I still remember the day my son's heartbeat echoed in that dark hospital room.

My wife was beaming. The technician asked if we were ready to hear it.

We smiled and eagerly said yes.

But when I heard it, I felt... nothing.

No joy. No connection. Just a deep, gnawing emptiness.

Actually, that's not quite true. I was annoyed. I wanted to go home. I had taken time off work for this. I had a glass of Scotch and a cigar waiting for me.

I had everything—a family, a future, a chance to be the father mine never was.

And yet I felt trapped. Anxious. Empty.

For the first time, I saw it clearly: I had never chosen fatherhood. It had always been forced upon me. Forced to be the "man of the house" as a child. Forced to take care of my mother. Forced to be a parent for my younger sister. Forced to sacrifice my needs for others.

I had fought so hard not to be like my father that I never saw the truth: I had been running from fatherhood my entire life.

I never truly reconciled with my father before he died. I had barely spoken to him in years. And then, at thirty, I found myself standing over his grave, realizing that his story had become mine. I was drinking more than ever. I resented my responsibilities. I felt the same distance from my son that I had felt from my father.

And then life gave me a gift.

When my wife and I separated, she asked me if I wanted to share custody of our son. She did not assume I wanted it. She did not push it on me.

She asked me what I wanted.

For the first time, fatherhood was not forced upon me. It was a choice. And that gave me my power back.

I had silently resented my son for existing, my wife for wanting kids to begin with, and myself for allowing it—because of a wound I'd been carrying for decades. The wound of never having been led by a father of my own.

By giving me the choice, my wife gave me the opportunity to say no. For the very first time, I allowed myself to contemplate being an absent father. Being everything I hated. I allowed myself to entertain my shadow, to treat it as a real possibility instead of resisting it.

And when I made the choice to be present in my son's life, I made the choice to give him something I never had.

Leadership.

---

My transformation was seeing that it was in my power to either perpetuate the wound or heal it. Everything I had experienced until that moment—all the pain and heartache, all the confusion, loss, and self-betrayal—would amount to nothing unless I alchemized it into meaning.

And so I did.

Having stepped into true worth. Having stepped into my true purpose. Having stepped into my true wholeness. I now looked nothing like the person I had been. I was no longer willing to compromise and live a life that required me to wear a mask. I wasn't going to betray, alienate, and estrange my true, authentic essence to acquire a false sense of value from someone else.

The moment I knew I was transformed was the moment I saw where my path had led me—and that none of it would be possible without every stumble along the way. I saw reality as it was. I understood the laws that govern the universe. I knew that I was an extension of the universe, existing for a moment in time simply to know itself better.

I hadn't just gained new knowledge. I had become someone who was capable of love—real, non-resistant love of what *is*. Learning to love myself made it possible for me to love others. Knowing my intrinsic value outside of what I do and what I provide made it possible to see the intrinsic value of others.

The journey wasn't about becoming someone new. It was about remembering someone old. The soul is always seeking wholeness—to reunite with its missing pieces.

My life had completely changed. Endless days of hell had become an infinite experience of heaven.

That's when I knew I held fire. And that I needed to share it with everyone who was ready to embrace it for themselves.

---

That's the Promethean moment.

Not a single flash of lightning. Not a burning bush or a voice from the sky. For me, it was the accumulation of every wound, every mask, every breakdown—finally alchemized into understanding. Into fire.

Your moment may look nothing like mine. Maybe it came through sobriety. Maybe it came through grief. Maybe it came through a near-death experience or a plant medicine ceremony or a conversation with a stranger that broke you open in a way you can't explain.

It doesn't matter how you got the fire.

What matters is that you have it. You know you have it. And you can't unknow it.

The weight of that knowledge—it feels like lightning in your bones. Like you've been given a secret that changes everything. Like you've escaped a prison and you can see the key that would free everyone else, right there within their grasp.

You *have* to open their eyes. You *have* to save the world.

That compulsion? That's not ego. That's not grandiosity. That's Prometheus looking down at the cold, dark world and knowing that what he holds in his hands could change everything.

The question is: what do you do with fire you've never held before?

That's what the rest of this book is about. But first, we need to talk about the fire itself—what it actually is, why it matters, and why the world needs you to stop hiding it.
