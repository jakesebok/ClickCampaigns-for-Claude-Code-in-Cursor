# Chapter 3: What You Hold Is Valuable

---

There's a line I keep coming back to. I've said it in coaching sessions, in talks, in quiet conversations over coffee, and in the middle of the night when I needed to hear it myself.

*The pain you feel is the war cry of your most authentic self, rebelling against a life that won't allow it to be free.*

Read that again. Slowly.

That ache in your chest—the one that shows up when you're doing work that doesn't matter, when you're performing a role that isn't you, when you're watching your life slide by while the real you stays locked behind glass—that's not weakness. That's not dysfunction. That's not something to be medicated away.

Tony Robbins put it this way: pain exists when your life conditions are out of alignment with your internal blueprint. The hypnotherapist Milton Erickson said patients are "out of rapport with their own unconscious"—they've lost the connection to the part of themselves that knows who they really are.

That ache? That's your fire, reminding you it exists. Your life doesn't need to be replaced. It needs to be *reclaimed*.

---

I need you to understand something, and I need you to understand it before we go any further in this book.

What you hold is valuable.

Not "could be valuable someday." Not "might be valuable if you get the right certification." Not "would be valuable if someone more qualified were holding it."

Valuable. Right now. As is.

Your transformation—the one you walked through, the one that broke you open and rebuilt you from the inside—that's not just a personal story. That's your credential. Your scars aren't just wounds. They're where your purpose lives.

Howard Thurman said it better than I ever could: *"Don't ask what the world needs. Ask what makes you come alive, and go do it. Because what the world needs is people who have come alive."*

You've come alive. That's the whole qualification.

---

But you don't believe it yet, do you?

I know you don't. Because I didn't either.

When I was in the thick of my transformation—reading Jung and Campbell, doing the shadow work, choosing fatherhood for the first time, learning to love myself—I knew something profound had happened. I *felt* it. I felt different in my body, in my relationships, in the way I saw reality.

But when someone would ask me what I did for a living, or when I'd try to articulate the value of what I'd been through, I'd freeze. Because the world doesn't have a box for what I was holding.

There's no degree in "surviving a dark night of the soul and coming back with wisdom." There's no certification for "I understand human transformation because I lived it." The credential isn't a piece of paper. It's the scar tissue itself. And that doesn't look good on a resume.

So you start to doubt.

Maybe this isn't as significant as I think it is. Maybe everyone goes through this. Maybe I'm just being dramatic. Maybe the fire I think I'm holding is actually just... warmth. Regular warmth that everyone has. Nothing special.

Here's the thing.

If you've been through genuine transformation—if you've peeled back the mask, confronted the shadow, died as the person you thought you were and been reborn—you are holding something that most people will never hold in their entire lives.

Not because they can't. But because they won't. Because the price is too high. Because the ordeal is too painful. Because most people, when they hear the call, refuse it and go back to their comfortable prison.

You didn't.

You walked into the fire. And you came back carrying something.

That something has a name. And its name is *experience*.

---

Let me tell you about a man named Thaddeus.

Thaddeus came to me stuck. He'd been through his own transformation—a deep personal reckoning that changed the way he saw everything. He knew he had something to offer. He knew it in his bones. But he couldn't figure out how to turn it into a business. He couldn't find the words. He couldn't justify charging money for something that felt sacred.

He was doing what most people in his position do: undervaluing his own fire because it didn't come with a certificate.

We worked together. We dug into his story, his genius, the specific flavor of transformation he'd lived through. And something shifted. He stopped comparing himself to people with traditional credentials and started owning what he actually carried—the lived wisdom that no one else could replicate because no one else had walked his specific path.

Once he saw it—once he *really* saw that his experience was his authority—everything clicked. The offer became clear. The words came. The clients appeared. Not because I gave him a formula, but because I helped him see what he was already holding.

That's the thing about fire. It's already in your hands. You just can't see it because you're too close.

---

We live in a world that worships credentials and dismisses experience. That hands out diplomas for information but has no ceremony for transformation. That trusts a piece of paper over a piece of a person.

And so when you show up holding fire—holding the kind of knowing that only comes from being broken open and rebuilt—you look around for the certificate. The badge. The thing that proves you're allowed to carry this.

There isn't one. And there never will be.

Because the value of what you hold isn't intellectual. It isn't informational. It isn't something that can be learned in a classroom or measured on an exam. What you hold is *experiential*. It lives in your body, in your story, in the specific way you see the world now that you've been to the underworld and back.

Nobody can teach what you know the way you know it. Because nobody walked your exact path. Nobody wore your exact mask. Nobody faced your exact dragon. Your fire is as unique as your fingerprint—and exactly as irreplaceable.

That's not a nice affirmation to put on a coffee mug. That's a market reality.

The people who need what you carry—and they're out there, more of them than you think—they don't need another coach with a certification. They need someone who's *been there*. Someone whose eyes tell the truth about what the journey costs. Someone who can look them in the face while they're falling apart and say, with conviction born from lived experience, "I know this feels like the end. It's not. I've been exactly where you are. And what's on the other side is worth everything you're going through."

No diploma gives you that.

Your scars do.

---

Here's the tension, though—and I want to name it honestly because pretending it doesn't exist won't help either of us.

You *know* what you hold is valuable. Somewhere deep down, beneath the doubt and the imposter syndrome and the voice that says *who are you?*, there's a part of you that *knows*. You've felt the impact of your presence on others. You've seen the look in someone's eyes when you said the exact thing they needed to hear. You've experienced the magnetic pull that happens when you speak your truth without filters.

But there's a gap between knowing and believing. And that gap is where most fire-wielders get stuck.

You know your fire is real. You just don't believe you're allowed to charge for it. Or lead with it. Or build your entire life around it.

Part of this is the money wound—we'll get to that later. Part of it is the mask—the old programming that says your value comes from what you *produce*, not who you *are*. And part of it is just the sheer strangeness of holding something precious that the marketplace hasn't told you how to price.

But consider this.

Every time you dim your fire to make it fit into a conventional mold—every time you call yourself a "mindset coach" instead of what you really are, every time you package your gift into something "sensible"—you're not just betraying yourself.

You're robbing the world of your unique gift.

The people who need your specific fire are out there right now, searching. Struggling. Wearing masks of their own. Dying a little more each day in jobs and relationships and lives that don't fit. And they need someone to show them the way out. Not someone with a generic framework. Someone with *fire*.

By not sharing yours, you're leaving them in the dark.

---

So let me say it one more time, as clearly as I know how.

Your transformation is your credential. Your pain is your authority. Your scars are not something to hide—they're where your purpose lives.

And the world doesn't need another person with a certificate and a clipboard.

The world needs you. Alive. On fire. Holding what you hold without apology.

The question isn't whether what you have is valuable.

It is.

The question is whether you're willing to let it be seen.

In the next chapter, we'll talk about the specific challenge of finding the place where your fire meets the world—that ephemeral, abstract, impossible-to-touch confluence of your unique genius and the market's need. It's elusive. It's maddening.

And it's more accessible than you think.
