# Chapter 20: What's Actually True

---

I want you to try something.

Take a step back from everything—the strategy, the offer, the fear, the fire—and ask yourself one question:

*What is actually true here?*

Not what you're afraid of. Not what the Voice of Reason says. Not what your identity thermostat is set to.

What's *actually* true.

---

When I ask my clients this question, something interesting happens. They pause. Not a thinking pause—a different kind. The kind where the machinery of performance momentarily shuts off and they're left standing in the quiet, honest center of their experience.

And what they find there is almost always different from the story they've been telling themselves.

The story says: *I'm not ready. I'm not good enough. My offer isn't strong enough. The market is too crowded. I don't have enough credentials.*

The truth says: *People are already being changed by my presence. My clients send me messages telling me I've shifted something fundamental in their lives. The ones who work with me don't just get better—they become different people.*

The story says: *I got lucky. It was a fluke. They're exaggerating. If they really knew me...*

The truth says: *This keeps happening. Over and over. With different people. In different contexts. The evidence is consistent, repeated, and real.*

Do you see the gap?

The gap between the story and the truth is where most fire-wielders live. They inhabit the story and visit the truth occasionally—when they get a glowing testimonial, when a session goes perfectly, when someone cries tears of gratitude. In those moments, they glimpse what's actually true. And then the story pulls them back.

---

This chapter is a reality check. Not the harsh kind—the generous kind. The kind that says: stop looking at the fears and start looking at the evidence.

What's actually true about your impact?

Think about the last five people you've worked with—formally or informally. The coaching clients, the friends you've helped, the people who've pulled you aside after a talk and said, "That thing you said changed something for me."

What happened to them? What shifted? What did they say? Not what you think they should have said. What did they *actually* say?

Write it down. Not the summary. The exact words.

*"I've never felt so seen in my entire life."*

*"You said something that I've been waiting to hear for years."*

*"I don't know what you did, but I feel different."*

*"My relationship with my partner changed after our conversation."*

*"I finally understand what I'm supposed to be doing."*

These aren't platitudes. These are data. These are the actual, measurable, real-world impact of your fire. And if you've been dismissing them—if you've been filing them under "nice things people say" instead of "evidence of my value"—then your self-concept is running on outdated software.

---

Aligning your self-image with reality is one of the most important and most neglected pieces of work in this entire journey.

Because your self-image dictates everything. It dictates what you charge. What you're willing to say. Who you're willing to serve. How big you're willing to dream. How visible you're willing to be.

If your self-image says "I'm an emerging coach who's still figuring things out," then everything you build will reflect that—tentative pricing, cautious messaging, defensive positioning. You'll play small because your self-image tells you that's your size.

But if your self-image catches up to the reality of your impact—if it absorbs the evidence, integrates the testimonials, accepts the fact that you are *actually changing lives*—then the business shifts. The pricing shifts. The messaging shifts. The energy shifts.

Not because you learned a new tactic. Because you updated the operating system.

Change your story, change your life. It really is that simple—and that hard. What we focus on, and how we emotionally respond to challenges, determine the external results we create. If you keep reinforcing fear, you keep reinforcing limitation. But when you recalibrate your focus—when you start looking at what's actually true instead of what the old story says is true—you activate a self-fulfilling mechanism of success.

---

This is the thermostat work from Chapter 12, applied in real time.

Back then, we talked about the identity thermostat as a concept. Now I'm asking you to do the work of actually adjusting it.

Here's the practice:

**Collect the evidence.** Go back through your messages, emails, conversations. Find every piece of feedback that tells you the truth about your impact. Not the qualified praise ("it was helpful!") but the real stuff. The moments where someone's voice cracked. Where they said something they'd never said to anyone before. Where the session ended and they sat in silence because something had moved.

**Let it in.** Don't read it and move on. Sit with it. Feel it in your body. Let the information land—not in your mind, where the Voice of Reason can debate it, but in your nervous system, where it can actually recalibrate.

**Stop explaining it away.** Every time you catch yourself discounting positive feedback—"they're just being nice," "it was an easy case," "I got lucky"—stop. Name what you're doing. And choose to believe the evidence instead of the story.

**Repeat.** This isn't a one-time exercise. It's a practice. A daily practice of choosing reality over narrative. Of letting the thermostat rise by one degree, and then another, and then another. Until your self-concept and your actual self are in the same room.

---

What's actually true is almost always better than what you're afraid of.

You are further along than you think. You are more impactful than you believe. You are carrying something that the world is actively looking for.

The evidence is there. It's been there for a while.

All you have to do is let yourself see it.
