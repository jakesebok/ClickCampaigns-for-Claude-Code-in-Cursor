# Chapter 19: Becoming the Person

---

There's a question I hear more than any other from the coaches, healers, and creators I work with.

"How do I grow my business?"

And my answer disappoints almost everyone the first time they hear it.

You don't grow your business. You grow *yourself*. The business follows.

---

This chapter isn't about strategy. It's not about marketing or funnels or offers or positioning. We've covered some of that, and there's more to come. But this chapter is about something deeper—something that, if you skip it, will undermine everything else you build.

It's about *becoming the person* who can hold and wield the power you have.

Because here's what nobody tells you: the business you're building requires a version of you that doesn't exist yet.

Not a smarter version. Not a more productive version. Not a version with better systems or more followers or a tighter elevator pitch.

A more *whole* version.

A version that can charge what you're worth without flinching. A version that can hold space for someone else's breakdown without absorbing it. A version that can speak your truth to a room full of strangers and not shrink. A version that can be visible, vulnerable, and powerful—all at the same time.

That person is who you're becoming. And the process of becoming them isn't about adding new skills. It's about releasing the parts of yourself that are still in the way.

---

This is what I mean when I say this work is much less about the *how* and much more about the *who*.

Most business education focuses on doingness. Do this marketing strategy. Do this launch sequence. Do this sales script. And those tools are useful—I'm not dismissing them. But they're useless in the hands of someone who hasn't done the ontological work.

Ontological. Fancy word. It means: related to the nature of being.

The ontological work is the work of becoming. Not becoming someone new—but becoming more fully who you already are. Stripping away the last residues of the mask. Dissolving the final layers of protection that kept you safe but now keep you small.

Michelangelo said it best. When asked how he created the statue of David, he replied: *"It is simple. I just remove everything that is not David."*

That's the ontological work. You're not sculpting a new you. You're removing everything that isn't.

This is the work that makes a coach not just competent but *powerful*. That makes a healer not just effective but *transformative*. That makes a creator not just skilled but *magnetic*.

It's the difference between performing your calling and *embodying* it.

---

Marshall taught me about this.

When I met Marshall—the chiropractor from Chapter 12—his business was successful on paper but miserable in practice. He had the clients, the revenue, the reputation. What he didn't have was the sense that any of it was actually *his*.

"The business feels like something I have to go *do*," he told me. "Like a second job. Like another performance."

When we worked together, we didn't talk much about business strategy. We talked about who he was becoming. We talked about the gap between the person running the practice and the person he was when he was at his best—when he was present, aligned, fully himself.

The work wasn't adding something. It was subtracting. Removing the obligations he'd inherited from other people's expectations. Dissolving the beliefs about what a "business owner" was supposed to look like. Letting go of the identity that said he had to be serious, buttoned-up, and corporate to be taken seriously.

When he stopped performing the role and started being himself—in his practice, with his patients, in his marketing, in his life—everything shifted.

"The business now feels more like an extension of me than something I have to go do," he said.

That's the shift. That's what becoming the person looks like.

---

Here's the uncomfortable truth about this stage.

You can't shortcut it.

You can't read a book about it and have it done. You can't watch a lecture and integrate it. You can't hire someone to do it for you. The becoming happens through *living*—through the daily practice of showing up as the person you're becoming before you feel ready. Before you feel worthy. Before the evidence arrives.

It looks like this:

Charging the price that scares you—before you believe you deserve it—and discovering that the right clients don't even flinch.

Having the conversation you've been avoiding—the one where you tell someone the truth about what you see—and watching their entire body relax because finally, *finally*, someone said the real thing.

Writing the post that makes you want to throw up with vulnerability—and watching it become the thing that brings your people to your door.

Standing in front of a group and being exactly who you are—not the polished version, not the prepared version, just *you*—and feeling the room shift because authenticity creates a gravity that performance never can.

Each of these moments is a vote for the person you're becoming. And each vote makes the next one easier.

---

The beautiful paradox of becoming the person is that you already are them.

We're not here to fix ourselves. We're here to *meet* ourselves.

You're not constructing a new identity. You're uncovering the one that's been there all along—buried under years of compromise, performance, and protection. The person who can run this business, who can hold this fire, who can lead these people—that person isn't in the future. They're in you, right now, waiting for permission to emerge.

Your job isn't to become someone new.

Your job is to stop being who you're not.

And part of that means learning where *you* end and *others* begin. I define healthy boundaries as *the distance at which I can love both you and me simultaneously.* When you hold fire, the temptation is to give everything—every ounce of energy, every hour, every piece of yourself—to the people you're serving. That's not generosity. That's self-abandonment. And self-abandonment will kill your fire faster than any external obstacle ever could.

---

I want to leave you with this.

Self-leadership is the foundation of all other leadership. You cannot guide someone to a place you haven't been willing to go yourself. You cannot hold space for someone's transformation if you're still hiding from your own. You cannot sell fire if you're still pretending you don't carry it.

The becoming isn't a prerequisite for the business. The becoming *is* the business. Every session you hold, every offer you make, every piece of content you create is an expression of the person you're becoming—or a performance of the person you're pretending to be.

Your clients will know the difference.

So will you.

Become the person. The business will follow.
