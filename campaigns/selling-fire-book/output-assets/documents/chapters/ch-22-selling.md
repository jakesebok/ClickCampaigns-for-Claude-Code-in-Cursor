# Chapter 22: Selling Fire Without Selling Your Soul

---

Let's talk about the thing that makes fire-wielders break out in hives.

Selling.

I know. Even the word feels wrong. It tastes like manipulation. It smells like the charlatans we spent a whole chapter being angry about. It conjures images of sleazy webinars and countdown timers and "BUT WAIT, THERE'S MORE."

You didn't walk through hell and come back carrying sacred fire so you could sound like an infomercial.

I get it. I felt the same way. For a long time, the word "selling" was synonymous with "becoming the person I despise."

But here's what I've learned: selling fire is nothing like selling anything else. And once you understand why, the whole thing stops feeling like a betrayal and starts feeling like what it actually is.

Service.

---

When you're selling coffee, you're convincing someone they want something they may not need. When you're selling gym memberships, you're leveraging guilt and aspiration. When you're selling software, you're solving a problem that has a clear, measurable ROI.

When you're selling fire, you're doing none of those things.

You're having a conversation with a human being who is in pain. You're listening—really listening—to what's beneath the surface of what they're saying. You're reflecting back to them the truth they've been afraid to see. And you're offering them a container—your First Elixir, or whatever your offer has become—where the transformation they need can actually happen.

That's not selling. That's coaching.

The best enrollment conversations I've ever had felt exactly like deep coaching sessions. Because they were. The person walks in with a problem. I listen. I ask questions. I reflect back what I hear. And at some point, if it's right—if I genuinely believe I can help—I say:

"I think I can help you with this. Here's what that would look like."

That's the whole sales pitch. There's no manipulation. No urgency. No manufactured scarcity. Just truth.

"I think I can help. Here's how."

---

Now, let me address the elephant: pricing.

You're going to have to charge money for your fire. Real money. Good money. And everything inside you is going to resist it.

Here's why you charge premium prices—and I mean *premium*, not bargain-basement, not "affordable for everyone," not priced so low that it signals you don't believe in what you're offering:

**Premium pricing protects the transformation.** When someone invests significantly in their own growth, they show up differently. They do the work. They take it seriously. They have skin in the game. Low-priced offers attract browsers. Premium offers attract people who are ready.

**Premium pricing protects you.** If you're charging too little, you need too many clients to survive. Too many clients means too much energy out. Too much energy out means you burn out. You burn out, the fire dies. The fire dies, nobody gets served. Charging premium means serving fewer people, more deeply, with more energy. Everybody wins.

**Premium pricing reflects the real value.** What you're offering isn't a product. It isn't a course. It isn't information. It's *transformation*. What is it worth to someone to finally understand who they really are? To stop wearing the mask? To build a life aligned with their purpose? The value of genuine transformation is not captured in a $97 price point.

I'm not saying gouge people. I'm saying stop apologizing for the value of what you carry. If what you do genuinely changes lives—and if you've done the work in this book, it does—then pricing it like a webinar replay is an insult to the fire and to the people who need it.

---

Here's how to stay lit when the old world calls you back.

Because it will. It always does.

You'll have a bad month. A client will drop off. A launch won't land. Your bank account will get uncomfortable. And the voice—the one we talked about in Chapter 11—will show up wearing its most convincing suit:

*See? This isn't working. You should have stayed in the real world. You should get a job. You should be practical. This was a nice dream, but it's time to grow up.*

When that voice comes—and it will—I need you to remember something.

The fire doesn't dim because the fire is failing. The fire dims because you're scared. And fear is the enemy of fire. Not failure. Fear.

A coach once told me something I'll never forget: *"You're fighting against what you fear instead of for what you love."*

That one sentence changed my trajectory. Because there's a profound difference between the two. When you fight against fear, every move is defensive—protecting, hoarding, avoiding loss. When you fight for what you love, every move is creative—building, reaching, inviting more.

The most powerful people in the world aren't fearless. They're just *focused*. Focused on the vision, not the threat. Focused on the fire, not the darkness.

Failure is just information. A launch that flopped is data. A client who left is data. A month with no revenue is data. None of that means the fire isn't real. It means the container needs adjustment. It means you haven't found the right people yet. It means the journey isn't over.

But if you let fear convince you to go back—to take the safe job, to shelve the dream, to put the fire on a shelf "until things settle down"—you know what happens. You lived it. It's in Chapter 6 of this book. The fire goes cold. The energy dies. And you spend years trying to resuscitate something that needed to be lived, not stored.

When the old world calls, thank it for its concern. And keep burning.

---

The secret to selling fire without selling your soul is this:

Never sell what you don't believe. Never promise what you can't deliver. Never package your truth into something unrecognizable just because the market says it'll sell.

If what you're selling is true—if the offer is real, the transformation is genuine, and the container is sound—then selling it is the most generous thing you can do. Because somewhere out there, right now, someone is struggling with the exact problem your fire was designed to solve. And they're waiting for someone to show up and say, "I've been where you are. I can help."

That's not manipulation.

That's love.

Sell the fire. Keep your soul. They were never in competition.
