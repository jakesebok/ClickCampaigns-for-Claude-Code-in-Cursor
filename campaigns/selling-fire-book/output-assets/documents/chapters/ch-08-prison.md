# Chapter 8: The Prison You Build

---

Here's something I need you to hear, because nobody else is going to say it this bluntly:

When you create a business that requires you to wake up every day and do something that compromises who you really are, all you've built is a prison.

It might have nicer furniture than the last one. It might pay better. You might have your name on the door instead of someone else's. But if the work makes you want to crawl back into bed by Tuesday, if the clients drain you instead of energize you, if you've traded one mask for another and the new one just fits slightly different—

It's a prison.

And you built it yourself.

---

This is the trap that almost nobody talks about in the coaching and entrepreneurship world. Everybody talks about leaving the job. Everybody celebrates the leap. The Instagram posts, the "I quit" announcements, the first-day-of-freedom selfies.

Nobody talks about what happens six months later when you realize you've recreated the exact dynamic you escaped—just with different branding.

Because here's the thing: when you leave a situation that was killing you, the first thing you do is try to build something that keeps you alive. You're not thinking about alignment. You're not thinking about purpose. You're thinking about survival. The bills. The debt. The panic that wakes you up at 4 AM.

So you compromise. Of course you do. You're human. You're scared. You've got real obligations to real people.

You take the coaching client who doesn't quite fit because they're willing to pay.

You build the offer that makes sense on paper even though it makes your soul go flat.

You call yourself something marketable—leadership consultant, mindset coach, executive performance strategist—because it sounds like something the world will buy.

And it works. Kind of. You get clients. You make some money. The bills get paid. Your family breathes a little easier.

But something's wrong. Something you can't quite name. A low-grade nausea that lives in the background of every workday. A heaviness when you open your laptop. A feeling that you've been here before.

You have been here before. This is the old world wearing new clothes.

---

I lived this. I described some of it in the last couple chapters—the "values-aligned performance coaching," the endless workshops I couldn't bring myself to build. But what I didn't fully describe was the specific, stomach-turning realization that came when I finally stopped and looked at what I was creating.

I was building a business. A real one. It had a name. It had a website. It had an offer structure and a target audience and a pricing model.

And it was everything I didn't want.

Not because it was bad. It wasn't. It was perfectly reasonable. Any business coach would have approved. It checked all the boxes—clear niche, defined outcome, scalable model.

But it wasn't mine. It wasn't the fire. It was a room-temperature version of my calling, optimized for market acceptance and stripped of everything that made it real.

I had escaped one prison only to build another.

The bricks were different—instead of someone else's vision, it was a watered-down version of my own. Instead of a boss I couldn't stand, it was clients I couldn't connect with. Instead of KPIs and quarterly reviews, it was content calendars and launch strategies for something I didn't believe in.

But it was still a prison. Because any structure that requires you to perform as someone you're not is a cage—regardless of whose name is on the lease.

---

Why do we do this? Why do smart, transformed, fire-wielding people keep building prisons for themselves?

Because the pattern is deep. Deeper than any transformation.

You spent years—maybe decades—learning to compromise. Learning to read the room. Learning to give people what they want instead of what's true. Learning to mold yourself into a shape that fits the expectations around you.

That pattern doesn't disappear because you had an awakening. It just gets more sophisticated. It goes from "I'll be who my parents want me to be" to "I'll build the business the market wants me to build." Same pattern. Fancier costume.

The mask doesn't vanish after one transformation. It shape-shifts. It becomes subtler, harder to detect, more convincing. The prison you build after your awakening doesn't look like the old one—it *can't*, because you'd recognize it immediately. So it disguises itself as something that's "close enough." Something that's "practical." Something that's "a stepping stone."

Here's how to know if you're in a prettier prison:

**The work is tolerable but not alive.** You can do it. You're competent. But when you're done for the day, you don't feel expanded—you feel drained. The energy goes out, not in.

**You avoid your own content.** You can't bring yourself to write the blog post, record the video, or build the course—because every time you sit down to create, something in you resists. Not because you're lazy. Because you're lying.

**You describe your work in someone else's language.** The words you use to explain what you do came from a marketing template, not from your heart. They "make sense" but they don't make you come alive.

**Your clients are fine, but they're not your people.** You serve them well enough. But you don't light up when you see their name on your calendar. You don't think about their transformation in the shower. You don't feel like you're doing sacred work.

**You feel guilty about wanting more.** You got out. You built something. You're "doing the thing." So why do you still feel restless? Why do you feel ungrateful? Why is the ache still there?

Because the ache isn't about what you have. It's about what you're withholding. The fire is still inside you, and you haven't let it out yet. Not really.

---

The good news—and there is good news—is that recognizing the prison is half the escape.

Most people never see it. They build the pretty prison, convince themselves it's freedom, and spend the rest of their lives managing the low-grade resentment that comes with half-lived purpose. They become successful-but-unfulfilled. Busy-but-empty. Respected-but-invisible.

But you see it. You're seeing it right now, as you read these words. That flicker of recognition—*Oh God, that's me*—that's not a failure. That's awareness. And awareness is the first step to demolition.

You don't have to burn the whole thing down tomorrow. Some of what you've built may actually be useful. Some clients may be the right ones. Some structures may hold real value. The prison isn't the business itself—it's the compromises embedded in it. The lies baked into its foundation. The places where you chose safety over truth.

You can renovate a prison into a home. But only if you're honest about which walls are load-bearing and which ones are cages.

---

I had a client named Larry. Chiropractor. Smart, capable, driven. He came to me saying he needed more leads. More marketing. More patients. The strategy problem.

But when we started digging, we found the *him* problem.

Larry was serving aging patients—pain management, chronic conditions. It was fine work. It paid the bills. But Larry had a story of his own: a serious concussion years earlier, a painful recovery, and an eventual return to full athletic function that changed the way he understood the body. His real fire? Helping athletes recover from injury. Young, vital people coming back from the brink.

Instead, he was adjusting elderly spines five days a week.

If he had actually achieved his stated goal—more leads for the existing practice—it would have been a prison that kept him from what he really wanted. Success at the wrong thing is just a more comfortable cage.

We aligned his business with his nervous system—and everything flowed smoothly from there.

---

I first heard this story from Steve Chandler, and it hasn't left me since.

An American executive arrives on a beautiful island and strolls down to the beach. He notices a fisherman rowing in to shore, his boat overflowing with fish.

"What are you doing?" asks the executive.

"I've been fishing because I love it," says the fisherman. "And now I'm going to have a barbecue on the beach with my friends. I'll play guitar and sing and hang out on the beach. And then in the evening, I'll dine with my wife under the stars."

"That's crazy," says the executive. "You've caught so many fish! I can invest in you and we'll sell the fish and make a ton of money."

"Why would I want to do that?" says the fisherman.

"Well, in a few months, we could invest the profits and buy a bigger boat and make even more money."

"Why would I want to do that?"

"Well, in a few years, we could invest the profits and open a factory right here on the beach and process our own fish and make even more money."

"Why would I want to do that?"

"Well, with all the profits from that, you could eventually retire early. And then you could go fishing just because you love it. And you could have a barbecue on the beach with your friends. And you could play guitar and sing and hang out on the beach. And in the evening, dine with your wife under the stars..."

That's the prison in its purest form. A decades-long detour that ends exactly where you already are—except now you've spent the best years of your life building something you never wanted in the first place.

If you don't consider your values when you engage with your career, you become the executive. You convince yourself that more is the answer. More clients. More revenue. More complexity. More hustle. Until one day you arrive at the destination and realize it looks exactly like the life you abandoned at the start—minus the years you'll never get back.

The fisherman already had the fire. He was already living it. The executive couldn't see that because his definition of success didn't have room for a man who was already free.

Don't build the factory. Build the life.

---

I want to leave you with this.

The risk of Selling Fire isn't that you'll fail. Plenty of people fail and recover. The real risk is that you'll succeed at the wrong thing. That you'll build something that works—that pays the bills, that impresses your friends, that looks like freedom from the outside—but that requires you to wake up every day and betray the fire you're holding.

That's not a business. That's a sentence.

You didn't survive your transformation—you didn't walk through the fire, lose everything, rebuild yourself from the ground up—so you could trade one performance for another.

You did it to become free. Actually free. The kind of free where your work and your truth are the same thing.

That's what we're building toward. But first, we need to talk about the most important shift that separates the people who stay stuck from the people who break through—the willingness to tell the truth about what you actually want.

Even when it scares you.

*Especially* when it scares you.
