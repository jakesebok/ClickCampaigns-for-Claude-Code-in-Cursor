# Chapter 6: Refusal of the Call
## The Quiet War with Your Day Job

---

Sunday afternoon. That's when it starts, right?

That's when the dread would start creeping in for me. Like I was getting a head start on being exhausted for a week that hadn't even started yet. The anxiety tightened in my chest, knowing I'd have to wear that heavy mask of "Project Manager" for another five days.

I'd wake up in the morning and my first thought wasn't gratitude or possibility. It was arithmetic.

*What day is it? Wednesday. Okay... two down. Three more to go.*

Three more days until the weekend. Three more days until I could be myself again.

I had divided my life into two parts: the hours I was working and the hours I was living.

The math was brutal. The living hours were losing. By a lot.

Every passing day, I knew I was squandering my gift. I knew I was trading fulfillment and impact for a consistent paycheck. I knew I needed to do something—*anything*—to get closer to living my purpose and making money from it.

But I was starting from scratch. The mountain in front of me was so big I couldn't even see the first step.

---

This is the quiet war.

The one that happens after you've been transformed but before you've found the courage to change your life. You've stolen fire from the gods. You've seen through the veil. You've touched the eternal...

And tomorrow morning you have a 9 AM meeting about workflow optimization.

For healers, it's pretending to care about corporate wellness programs when you know deep down they're just band-aids on bullet holes.

For creators, it's designing someone else's vision while yours withers in a notebook you're too exhausted to open.

For coaches, it's sitting in meetings about employee engagement and productivity—knowing exactly what would actually transform these people's lives—and having to bite your tongue until it bleeds because your solution doesn't support the bottom line.

I lived this war for two years. Two years of knowing exactly who I was meant to be while pretending to be someone else. Two years of holding fire in my bones while discussing KPIs. Two years of silent resentment building like steam in a pressure cooker.

Joseph Campbell would call this pain *The Call to Adventure*.

In stories, it's the moment where the would-be hero's ordinary life is interrupted: Luke receives a message in an astrodroid, Katniss Everdeen's sister is chosen as tribute, Frodo Baggins inherits a mysterious ring.

In real life, it can be a breakup. A book you read. A diagnosis. It's simply something that changes the way you see your world. Something that reveals what's possible.

And when you've seen what's possible and still live in the mundane? It feels icky. Like wearing shoes that are two sizes too small. Like speaking a language that isn't yours.

I refer to the Call in more dramatic terms. I say that this dis-ease is the war cry of your most authentic self, rebelling against a life that won't allow it to be free.

When you're not living your calling, you'll hear that war cry in every new email notification and meeting reminder.

---

Here's the thing about refusing the call: it doesn't make the call go away.

It just makes everything harder.

The Bible has a story about this. Jonah was commanded by God to go to Nineveh—to deliver fire, essentially. A message the people needed to hear. Jonah didn't want to go. Too scary. Too big. Too much. So he ran the other direction. Got on a boat sailing away from his purpose.

You know how that worked out. The sea swallowed him. He ended up in the belly of a whale—stuck in darkness, with nowhere to run, finally forced to confront the thing he'd been avoiding.

The call doesn't go away when you refuse it. It follows you. And the longer you run, the darker the belly gets.

The universe has a sick sense of humor about this.

You tell yourself you'll keep the job for security while you build your real work on the side—and you *should* do that, by the way; more on that later.

You download business books. You follow entrepreneurs on social media. You start sketching out websites at night.

You convince yourself you're making progress while tiptoeing into the shallow end, never risking anything, gaining no real ground in the process.

Don't get me wrong—there's a place for testing the waters. There's a place for small experiments that build confidence. Do it. Run them. But never mistake the truth: life change requires bold action.

---

I wouldn't be where I am now without some of those first, fledgling steps at the edge of the water.

I remember reading Alex Hormozi's *$100M Offers*. A friend suggested it and the audiobook was ninety-nine cents—a deal so good I would have needed to be stupid to say no. Surprisingly, I didn't hate it. I had a visceral aversion to sales and marketing of any kind at that time. I didn't want to be "that guy."

That book actually reframed business for me as an exchange of value instead of what I thought it was—some shyster trying to get you to pay more than you should for something you don't need. Reading it allowed me to stop the war inside me that was forcing me to choose between being "good" and being "successful."

So I started making exit plans.

I wasn't ready to go all in on my big, hairy, audacious dream yet, but I thought I could build a passive income side hustle to create some cash flow and free up my time. *Then*, I told myself, I could make the real impact.

I started building lead generation websites on the side. The plan was simple: leverage the digital marketing skills I already had from my day job to create websites for services like roofing or landscaping, optimize them to rank on Google, and sell the leads to business owners for a monthly fee.

It seemed easy enough. Made some money. It was hands-off enough.

And the best part was that I could do it without ever having to put my face or my name behind it. Because secretly, I was still ashamed. Embarrassed to be creating a business. *Who was I* to start a business? What made me so arrogant to think I could be successful?

I was so embarrassed that I remember receiving the LLC registration papers in the mail and rushing to the mailbox so my housemates wouldn't see them. And when they did eventually see the endless credit card offers my new business received—so many trees!—I refused to tell them what it did. I didn't want to be known as the guy who constructed a heartless lead gen funnel.

I wanted to be the heart-forward transformational coach who made an *impact*.

And that was a truth I couldn't deny. Because I found myself in the website builder I'd been using for the lead gen sites, constructing something else entirely—a website for coaching people through their own Hero's Journey. A random outpouring of creative energy I hadn't even put a shape around yet. According to the homepage in front of me, I used mythology to help people live lives of fullness, passion, and bliss.

I called it Monomyth Journeys. As if anyone was going to be interested in *that*.

Regardless, it was the seed of what would become my real work.

I just planted it really deep.

---

Meanwhile, something strange was happening at my day job.

The more I learned about business and offers, the more valuable I became to my company. I brought my systems-building gift to our operations. I felt appreciated. Seen. Useful.

Over the next year, we stabilized. Brought in new team members. I convinced myself I could coast now. Put in the bare minimum. Maybe *that* was the good life. Maybe I didn't need a big, profound, mission-driven life. I just needed a place where I could settle in and disappear.

But then something happened that I didn't expect.

I started to feel like Bill Murray in *Groundhog Day*. Same meetings. Same conversations. Same performance of caring about things that didn't matter. I'd stare at my screen for hours, unable to complete a single task.

It was miserable.

Then my boss decided it was time to scale.

I saw a new opportunity to feel alive—a new carrot on the end of a new stick to chase. I revamped our business model from the ground up. Took the *$100M Offers* playbook and created a new offer. All of our current clients transitioned, and on January 1st of the next year, we'd already generated 50% more revenue than the entire previous year.

I felt *alive* again.

I hired a team I loved. We built cool projects. We served our customers well.

By July, we had contracts for the next three years that amounted to increasing the previous year's revenue by 18.5x.

By November, I was fired.

Yes, you read that right.

Remember the universe? That sick sense of humor?

---

I'm getting ahead of myself. Let's go back to August.

The worst part wasn't what it cost me. The worst part was how it was impacting the people I cared about.

Earlier that year, we'd begun to scale so quickly that we needed more resources. I hired a strategist. She was brilliant, creative, part of my tribe—perfectly aligned with the vision I'd constructed in my head.

The problem? When I interviewed her a year before, the vision I pitched was my fantasy version of the company, not the real one. I'd twisted the company's mission to match my values. Created an imaginary culture that only existed in my imagination so I could sleep at night and come to work each morning.

The war inside my heart was leaking out and getting everything messy.

She thought she was joining the company I'd imagined. And it worked—for a while.

During that first year, it was mostly she and I running things. The owner had disappeared to "become a CEO." Executive coaching, masterminds, workshops. When he returned, he was a brand new person. I didn't even recognize him.

When he stepped in and she experienced the *real* company—not the one I'd invented and invited her into—her latent autoimmune condition flared up. The stress put her out of work for days at a time.

One August day, she called me crying. She said she couldn't do it anymore. She put in her notice.

She knew it wasn't worth her health to pretend. She had the courage to do what I had not.

My double life hadn't just cost me.

It had literally made someone I was responsible for sick.

---

We continued on. Pulled together. Delivered for our clients without her. But now, something truly valuable was gone. There was nowhere safe left to hide, nowhere I could be truly me, and it was showing.

I had pulled the owner aside a few weeks earlier. My team had been asking me what was wrong between us. His frustration with me had become visible—exploding at me during team meetings.

I vulnerably asked him if he was angry with me about something. Then I braced for impact.

He closed his eyes. Chose his words carefully. To this day, I respect the hell out of his response.

"Jake, I don't think I'm angry at you. But I am so frustrated. You're so talented. You're so smart. So charismatic and capable. But you're just not in the right place, and I don't know where the right place for you is."

He said he'd do anything. Create a new position. Support me however he could. And then he finished his thought:

"I've been wracking my brain, but I can't figure out where you fit. Do you know?"

I did.

I knew instantly. My disguise had been seen right through. I wasn't hiding it as well as I thought. My heart had gone somewhere else. And with it, my energy, my engagement, and my productivity.

I had discovered transformational coaching. I knew it was my next big challenge. The thing I was actually meant to do.

You might think, dear reader, that this was the moment where I told him the truth. That I said, "Actually, I want to be a coach. I want to develop my skills and provoke awareness and inspire change."

Sometimes I imagine that I said that too.

But I was too afraid. I was afraid that if I told the truth, I'd lose my security. He'd know for a fact that project management wasn't for me and wouldn't trust me to support his company any longer.

So I kept pretending.

Poorly.

By then, I could barely complete simple tasks. I'd sit at my screen for hours, staring. Not procrastinating. Not distracted. Just empty. There was no energy left for the performance.

Jim Carrey once said that depression is your body saying, "*Fuck you. I'm done. Done playing the role. Done wearing the costume. I don't want to be this character anymore. It's too much for me.*"

That's what was happening. My body literally stopped giving me energy to play the role.

---

Fast forward to November. Friday morning. 7 AM. An email.

Fired.

My first thought: financial security.

My second thought: *I'm finally free.*

I threw a party that night and called it "The Universe Moved Me."

Because sometimes the universe has to do for you what you can't do for yourself. Like Jonah in the belly of the whale—I'd refused the call the easy way. So it had to happen the hard way. The whale spit me out exactly where I needed to be. Whether I liked it or not.

---

The cosmic joke?

Within a year, AI made my previous company's core offerings largely obsolete. The owner pivoted. You know what the company does now?

Coaching. For founder-led organizations.

The very thing I'd been called to do. The very thing I was too afraid to speak.

If I'd told the truth, I could have been instrumental in building that. Instead, I got fired for being unable to complete basic tasks while the answer was burning in my chest.

---

Here's what two years of refusing the call cost me.

When I first transformed, I was on fire. I remember making videos with passionate urgency—videos I never posted. I was speaking truths that were alive in my bones. The energy was infinite.

Two years later? The current was gone.

When I finally tried to coach, it felt like reciting lines from a play I'd seen once, a long time ago.

That's why my first coaching practice failed. It was already dead on the vine.

Worse, I had to build without income—and that changes the game. When you create from fear and scarcity, you build defensive fortresses. You run from what you hate instead of toward what you love.

But the worst cost?

My family had to bear the burden. They suffered for a year because I wouldn't speak my truth.

The thing about transformation is that it is forged in fire. It is born of pain. It requires death. Where I had transformed the first time by myself, this time I had a partner and children to care for. This time, I didn't go to the underworld alone. I took them with me. They suffered for my delay.

Hurting yourself is one thing.

Hurting those you love because you're too afraid to live your purpose?

That's unforgivable.

---

So if you're sitting at your desk right now, feeling that Sunday dread on a Tuesday, knowing you're meant for more but afraid to leap—let me tell you what I learned:

**You won't ever bring your true fire to something that doesn't measure up to the vision you know is right.** It is Truth itself that is inviting you to be a conduit for it on this plane. You can't fool the Truth. It knows when you're lying.

**The war ends one of two ways.** You choose to leave, or the universe chooses for you. Either way, you leave.

The only question is how much of yourself you'll lose in the meantime.

The only question is how many others will suffer while you delay.

The only question is whether you'll walk out the door or be pushed through it.

Okay... that was three questions. I take it back. There are only three questions.

When you know what you need to do—the thing that would bring you true fulfillment—you become resentful of anything that stands in the way. Including your day job. You have seen the potential beneath the mask. The energy required to keep up the act is too much for you now. You will become a version of yourself that you hate if you continue to pretend after you've seen the truth.

The fire you're holding? It's not meant to be hidden under quarterly reports and team meetings.

My definition of rock bottom is the moment when the pain of staying the same outweighs the fear of change.

And here's the mechanism that makes it worse: every time you choose the familiar, you reset the threshold for how much pain you're willing to endure in order to avoid the discomfort of the unknown. The tolerance builds. The cage shrinks. What was unbearable yesterday becomes "manageable" today. Until one day you wake up and realize you've been managing your way through a life that's barely recognizable.

Every day you refuse the call is a day you choose to make your lesson a little bit harder to learn.

I want to create a world of people doing what sets their souls on fire. I always say that I can take credit for the moments I chose to act when I didn't have to, but I cannot take credit for becoming aware that I needed to act to begin with. So I'm going to do my best to make damned certain that if you do wait another day to act, it's not because you didn't know you had the choice. And it isn't because you didn't have the tools to move forward.

After this, it's on you.

---

One more thing. There's a distinction that matters here—one I wish I'd understood earlier.

Everyone talks about the Refusal of the Call. That's what this chapter is about. You hear the call and you won't go. Jonah runs from Nineveh. You stare at the ceiling on Sunday night.

But Campbell mapped another refusal—one that comes *after* the transformation. He called it the Refusal of the Return. That's when you've been to the mountaintop. You hold the fire. And you won't bring it back. You keep it for yourself. You tell yourself you're "still integrating." You meditate and journal and read another book. But you never descend the mountain.

The Refusal of the Call keeps you in the old world. The Refusal of the Return keeps you on the mountaintop—transformed but useless to the people below who are freezing in the dark.

Both are prisons. Different altitudes. Same bars.

If you're reading this book, you've probably struggled with both.

---

But here's the cruelest part of leaving—the part nobody warns you about.

You've finally freed yourself. You've escaped the matrix. You've walked through the fire and come out the other side.

And your landlord still wants rent.

That's next.
