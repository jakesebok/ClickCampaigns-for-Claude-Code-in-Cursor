# Chapter 5: Effing the Ineffable
## The Problem with Words

---

If you're anything like I was after the transformative experience that changed the way I saw my history and altered the course of my future, the lightning you hold is burning through your bones right now.

You've finally seen reality for the first time. And it's not the two-dimensional shadows on the cave wall you thought it was. It's the real, three-dimensional, capital-T Truth. You're looking at everything as if it's brand new—because the wisdom you now possess has changed everything. Past, present, and future.

T.S. Eliot immortalized this moment better than anyone:

> *We shall not cease from exploration*
> *And the end of all our exploring*
> *Will be to arrive where we started*
> *And know the place for the first time.*

It is not the place that has changed. It's you. And you can never go back to being who you were before the journey took place.

---

You're awake now in a world of sleepwalkers.

It can be lonely here—feeling like you share space with others but not the same experience. Every conversation is tinged with a strange kind of pain, because you can see the invisible chains that people not only wear but willingly shackle themselves into daily.

Every "how are you?" becomes an opportunity to scream, "*Wake up!*"

You can see the key that would unbind them. It's right there, within their grasp. All you have to do is show them. You just have to make them see.

But the words fumble on your tongue and fall dead before they ever reach the ears of their intended target.

---

Here's the thing about stealing fire from the gods: language will always betray you.

The truth you hold is ineffable—it's pre-linguistic. The moment you try to capture your fire in words, you change it. You reduce it. Your words sterilize it to the point that what the other person receives is nothing at all like the powerful truth that was revealed to you.

I learned this the hard way. Over and over.

Somebody would ask me at a networking event—or at a dinner party, or in a casual conversation—"So what do you do?" And I'd feel this swell of energy rise up in me. I *knew* what I did. I knew it in my cells. I knew the lives I was changing and the doors I was opening and the fire I was holding.

And what came out of my mouth sounded something like: "I'm a... transformational... I help people... it's kind of like coaching, but more... I use mythology and the hero's journey to help you... find your purpose..."

And I'd watch their eyes glaze over. Every time. The words I used were accurate but they were *dead*. They described the thing without containing any of its heat.

Try explaining a sunset to someone who's never seen one. Now imagine your entire livelihood depends on it.

That's the Promethean burden. Holding truth that evaporates the second you try to hand it to someone else.

---

See, there's a world of difference between information and transformation.

Teachers inform. Mentors transform.

This distinction matters more than almost anything else in this book. Because it explains the core frustration you've probably been feeling—and it points to the way through.

When you were transformed, it wasn't because someone handed you a fact sheet. It wasn't because you read the right three sentences in the right order. It was because something *happened* to you. An experience. A moment of collapse or clarity or both at once. Something broke open that could not be broken open by information alone.

The fire that transformed you was a *lived experience*.

But when you try to share it, you default to information. You tell your story. You explain the concepts. You lay out the framework. You present the ideas that changed you—expecting them to land with the same force.

They don't.

Because the question isn't *how to share your revelation*. The question is *how to recreate that experience in another person*. And those are two profoundly different things.

Joseph Campbell called this problem "mistaking the symbol for the reference."

Language is nothing but symbols. And those symbols are nothing like the thing to which they refer. The word "tree," for instance, is a series of squiggles on a page or a short burst of sound that rhymes with "free." But what a tree really *is*—the roots drinking from underground rivers, the bark breathing, the canopy reaching for light it will never fully hold—that's something else entirely.

You can spend your whole life describing the tree. Nobody's going to feel the shade.

---

So then, how do you explain color to someone who's only known grayscale? How do you describe three dimensions to someone living in flatland?

This is the problem that sends most fire-wielders into one of two traps.

**Trap one: you water it down.** You take the ineffable, reduce it to something simple and palatable, and offer that instead. Performance coaching. Mindset training. Goal setting. It's not wrong—it's just not *it*. It's the shadow of the fire. The husk. The brochure for a journey you can't put on a brochure.

**Trap two: you go full mystic.** You refuse to compromise, so you speak in the language of the experience itself—the language of awakening, of soul, of sacred purpose. Which is beautiful and true and means absolutely nothing to someone who hasn't been there yet. You sound like you're speaking in tongues at a board meeting.

Both traps have the same root: you're trying to transfer fire using the wrong medium.

Information doesn't carry fire. *Experience* carries fire.

But here's the thing—and this is the good news, the real good news: you don't have to figure out how to put the ineffable into words. You have to figure out how to create moments of recognition. Moments where the other person's own fire gets sparked. Not by what you *said*, but by what you *created* for them to experience.

This isn't an insurmountable obstacle. It's a skills gap.

And skills can be learned.

---

I learned firsthand during my undergraduate work that being the world's foremost expert on a subject does not qualify you to teach it. I've sat in lecture halls watching brilliant minds fail spectacularly at transferring even a fraction of their understanding to the students in front of them. They had the fire. They had decades of fire. They just had no idea how to create the conditions for someone else to feel its warmth.

Without skills, you just keep telling your story in different ways, expecting it to be as revelatory for others as it was for you. You're sharing information when you need to be creating experience. You're teaching when you need to be provoking. You're describing fire when you need to be lighting one.

The gap between *having* the fire and *being able to transfer it* is where most healers, coaches, and creators get stuck. It's not that their gift isn't real. It's not that the market doesn't need it. It's that they haven't yet developed the skills—the very specific, learnable skills—of translating lived transformation into an experience that someone else can walk through.

Coaching skills. Communication skills. The ability to ask a question that cracks someone open. The ability to hold space while someone falls apart and help them see that the falling apart is the whole point. The ability to design a container—a process, a program, a conversation—where fire can be kindled without you having to directly hand it over.

These are not gifts you're born with. They are crafts you develop. And they are absolutely within your reach.

---

Here's why this matters right now, at this point in the book.

We've spent the first four chapters establishing what you hold. The fire is real. The transformation is real. Your scars are your credentials, and your experience is more valuable than you think.

But the fire means nothing if you can't pass it on.

And passing it on is harder than anyone tells you—not because you're broken, not because you're not ready, but because the very nature of what you hold resists being captured.

So what do you do?

You learn the skills. You practice the craft. You stop trying to *tell* people what you know and start learning how to *create* the conditions where they discover it for themselves.

That's the work ahead.

Joseph Campbell said, *"The cave you fear to enter holds the treasure you seek."*

Every fire-wielder stands at the mouth of that cave.

But first? Before we get to the skills, we need to talk about what gets in the way. Because between where you are right now—holding fire, struggling to explain it, desperate to share it—and the life where you actually get paid to do this work... there's a gauntlet. A set of pitfalls so common, so predictable, and so devastating that they stop most fire-wielders before they ever get started.

You need to know what's coming.

Let's walk into the dark.
