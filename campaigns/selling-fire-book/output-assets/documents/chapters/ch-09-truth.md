# Chapter 9: The Importance of Telling the Truth

---

That first year of business, I kept lying to myself about what I really wanted to do.

I was following the money. Trying to create offers for clients I didn't want to work with. Pitching workshops for businesses I didn't actually care about. Every time I sat down to build something—a website, a slide deck, an outline for a talk—the writer's block hit me like a wall. Not the kind where you can't find the right words. The kind where your entire body goes limp. Where you stare at the screen and feel *nothing*.

That should have been a sign. It was, actually. A flashing neon sign. But listening to my intuition wasn't yet a skill I had refined.

The truth was that I was trying to figure out a way to cover up my real ambitions. I wanted to wake people up. I wanted to help them quit the jobs that were slowly killing them. I wanted to open a door to a bigger, more powerful, more impactful life for everyone I spoke to.

But I felt like I had to lie about this and do it covertly—because the only people willing to pay me were their bosses.

I had traded one mask for another. And I was dangerously close to constructing a brand new prison made of different bricks—but just as capable of making me feel like a slave.

---

I was completely broke. Twenty thousand dollars in credit card debt on cards I'd opened only months before. Chasing down an avenue of business that only seemed interesting because of the dollar signs attached to it.

I hadn't truly dialed in the messaging or the offer for the thing I thought I wanted at the beginning. Which meant I'd lost belief in myself—belief that what I actually wanted to do was capable of making dollars and cents.

Then I got an opportunity.

A guy named Matt—a young realtor—flashed something shiny in front of my eyes. I'd met him at a local networking event where I was stumbling through my pitch about helping men become better men. That's how bad my messaging was. I knew in my heart that I wanted to help them become better fathers, better husbands. I knew I wanted to help them see that they were on their own hero's journey and walk through it together to find the person they truly were behind the mask.

But I didn't know how to talk about it.

Whatever I said, though, it resonated with Matt. He called me up and told me his story. When he was young in the business, he was just taking whatever leads the office gave him. Spinning his wheels. No direction. He probably would have burned out—but he got a coach. The coach helped him create a vision, set milestones, build actionable steps toward that vision. Now Matt was the guy running a team of twenty young realtors. All guys. And he wanted what his coach had done for him—done for them.

"Jake, I've got twenty guys. What if you met with each of them every other week? Ten guys a week, maybe two per day. We'd pay you about a hundred fifty an hour. That's fifteen hundred a week."

My brain did the math before he finished the sentence.

*$6,000 a month. That's enough to pay my bills. I absolutely have to land this.*

So I worked up an offer. And immediately, my survival instincts kicked in. I started telling Matt about my background in project and operational management. Setting OKRs and KPIs. Building project roadmaps. How I'd helped my previous company increase revenue by 18.5x in nine months.

All of it true.

None of it what I actually wanted to do.

I just saw that need for security, and I was willing to compromise everything else to achieve it.

I was lying to myself. I was lying to the universe. I was lying to Matt.

And here's the part that stopped me cold when I finally saw it clearly: it felt like *home*.

Not the good kind. The familiar kind. The pattern I'd been running my entire life—the one where I learned that I needed to wear a mask, needed to do things I didn't believe in, in order to get what I needed. In order to be secure.

And here it was again, wearing different clothes but walking the same walk.

I pulled the door wide open and I ran in.

---

Matt said he'd have to sell his partner on the idea. Said he'd get back to me within a week.

A week went by. I reached out. No return calls. No messages.

Eventually I got a text. It was a no-go. He blamed it on the partner.

But the idea had taken root. That feeling of *home*—that dangerous, familiar feeling—had me hooked. I wanted it so badly that I turned it into a new brand. I called it Aligned Power. I wasn't willing to just be a business coach. That's not what I wanted. I wanted it to feel *something* like what my heart desired.

So I was going to create an alignment roadmap for team members. Make sure that whatever vision they created for their future, they never compromised on their non-negotiables. They'd fuel themselves with what actually motivated them.

It doesn't just have to be realtors, I told myself. It could be high performers of any kind.

And then I thought about someone I knew—a named partner at a law firm. Called him up. Told him what I was doing.

He sounded interested. Said it sounded really cool. Then came the hesitation.

"Jake, I'd love to do something like that, but I've got to sell it to my partners. And I just don't think I can convince them that creating a values-aligned roadmap is good enough. Sounds to me like if my employees started doing that, they might stop working. They might go off and meditate on mountaintops and talk to the Dalai Lama."

See, he had a vested interest in his people *not* getting what they wanted. Not finding alignment. He wanted them to keep showing up and performing more. That was the quiet part said loud.

My response was that they'd still perform highly—they'd just do it at a sustainable level. He'd reduce turnover. Increase productivity. Improve well-being.

He listened. Then he told me what he actually needed.

"Jacob, what we really need is higher performance. I'm a personal injury lawyer. When we go to court, we either win everything or we lose everything. My young guys—they're half in, half out. They're settling for two million when they should be settling for five. There's money left on the table."

Then the question:

"Do you think you could do some work in, like, performance coaching? Mindset coaching?"

And there it was. Another open door to compromise.

"Yeah," I said. "Absolutely. I can do some mindset coaching. Help your high performers become even higher performing. But I'll do it in a values-aligned way."

I called it *values-aligned performance coaching*.

Even the words coming out of my mouth felt gross. They felt cheap. They felt empty.

I couldn't have cared less about performance. I've never been a quantity guy. I've always been a quality guy. I cared deeply about stories. About values. About human experiences. But the moment you start quantifying those things, I just... could not care less.

---

I stayed stuck in that offer for months.

Met with every person I could think of—friends, acquaintances, business owners around town. Told them all what I was doing. They'd nod, smile, ask if I could create a workshop. A lunch-and-learn. They told me it'd be a great opportunity.

But the whole time, I felt like a fraud. I was trying to convince them they wanted my services even though *I* didn't even know what my services were.

I hadn't defined what an alignment roadmap really looked like. I hadn't built a process. I hadn't developed a real offer—because nobody had offered to pay me yet, and that was my singular criterion for motivation. Without a true offer, I couldn't speak about it with conviction.

They could tell.

They could tell it wasn't real. They could tell I wasn't prepared. And every single time I sat down to develop the workshop, to build the roadmap, to figure out what this coaching program actually looked like—

I completely lost all my steam.

I was so disinterested and unmotivated by the prospect of creating something that wasn't aligned with who I was. Once again, I was sabotaging myself. Sabotaging my progress.

And what became extraordinarily clear to me was that what I'd been trying to hide was the very desire I could never shut down.

It was a desire to help others turn their passions—their unique gifts, the fire they stole from the mountaintop—into something they could offer others. Something they could support their families with. Something they could wake up every day excited to do.

I wanted them to quit their jobs. I wanted to wake them up, shake them up, break their reality. I wanted to call them to the greatest adventure of their lives—to figure out who they really were, to go through the metamorphosis, the great ordeal. I wanted to walk them through it myself. I wanted to see them change.

And instead, I was telling their bosses I wanted to increase retention. Reduce turnover. Help with productivity.

Every word falling completely empty as it came out of my mouth.

---

Here's the thing about lying.

You think you're being strategic. Smart. Realistic. You're reading the room, giving people what they want to hear, positioning yourself in a way that makes sense to the market.

But your soul doesn't care about your market positioning.

There's a scene in the show *Bad Monkey* where Vince Vaughn's character gives a speech about lies: "If you lie to get out of consequences, the one person you can never fully convince is you... and that's no way to live." I'd never heard it put more simply.

Your soul knows when you're lying. And it stages a revolt.

That's what the writer's block was. That's what the lost steam was. That's what the flat affect and the empty stares were. Not a lack of motivation. Not a lack of discipline. A lack of *truth*.

Your resistance isn't a bug. It's a feature. It's your soul's way of saying: *This isn't it. This isn't what wants to be created through you.*

Brené Brown makes a distinction between *belonging* and *fitting in*: "If I get to be like me, I belong. If I have to be like you, I fit in." That's the crispest definition I've ever heard. Belonging is when you're accepted for who you really are. Fitting in is when you're accepted for being like everyone else—for performing the version of yourself that the room seems to want.

I was fitting in everywhere. I was belonging nowhere.

I was so desperate for someone to say yes—to a deal, to a workshop, to a lunch-and-learn—that I was willing to reshape myself into whatever the room needed. Performance coach for realtors. Mindset trainer for lawyers. Workshop facilitator for whoever would pay.

But none of those yeses would have been *mine*. They would have been for the character I was playing. For the mask.

Rejection as a filter works both ways. When someone says no to the real you, they were never your person. But when someone says yes to the fake you? That's worse. Because now you have to keep performing to keep the thing you never actually wanted.

---

That was the moment when I knew I needed to ask a different question.

Not: *What am I capable of that other people are willing to pay for?*

But rather: *What wants to be created through me?*

Or, even better: *What needs to be created through me?*

When I started asking that question, everything changed.

Not overnight. Not with fireworks. But the ground shifted. The writer's block loosened. The energy came back. Because I wasn't fighting my own current anymore. I was finally swimming with it.

The irony is that the thing I'd been hiding—the desire to help people transform their lives and turn that transformation into their vocation—was the very thing people eventually paid me for. The thing I was most afraid to say out loud turned out to be the most magnetic thing about me.

Because here's what I've learned: you can't hide your real fire behind a sensible offer and expect anyone to feel the heat.

People don't buy frameworks. They don't buy methodologies. They buy *conviction*. And conviction is impossible to fake.

When I stopped trying to be the values-aligned performance coach and started telling people what I actually wanted to do—that I wanted to help them find the courage to live their purpose and build a life around it—something remarkable happened.

They leaned in.

Not everyone. Some people looked at me like I had three heads. Some people stopped returning my calls. Some people thought I'd lost my mind.

And that was the filter doing its work.

The ones who stayed? The ones who lit up? Those were my people. The ones I was always meant to serve. The ones who'd been waiting for someone to say the thing I'd been too afraid to say.

---

If you're sitting in a version of this right now—if you're building an offer that makes sense on paper but makes your soul go numb—I want you to hear me.

You're not broken. You're not lazy. You're not lacking discipline or motivation.

You're lying. And your resistance is trying to tell you.

The question isn't *What will the market buy?*

The question is *What wants to be created through me?*

Ask that question. Sit with it. Let it answer.

The market you're looking for is looking for you too. But they can't find you while you're wearing someone else's face.

The next chapter is about what happens when you look up from your own struggle and realize you're not the only one who's been compromising. There's a whole industry of people selling transformation they've never experienced—and they're winning.

That's going to piss you off. Good. Let's use it.
