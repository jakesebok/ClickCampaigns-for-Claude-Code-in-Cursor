Brand Concept 08 — Detonation

Logo Exploration

Three mark directions. Each one holds the same philosophy: corporate structure, orange detonation. The contrast is the brand.

Logo Concept
Mark A
The Slash Wordmark
"Aligned Power" set in composed Cormorant Garamond. A single orange stroke cuts diagonally through the letter P — not decorating it, cutting through it. The typographic detonation. The word is still legible. The structure is intact. But something has already broken through.

Design rationale

The slash is the entire brand philosophy in a single stroke. Cormorant Garamond at 700 weight is serious, composed, authority-coded — the corporate establishment. The orange slash cuts through the P in "Power" from bottom-left to top-right, like a signature, like a knife, like a decision being made. The word is still perfectly readable. The structure survives. But the mark has been made — and can't be undone.

  • On light backgrounds: ink type, orange slash
  • On dark backgrounds: white type, orange slash (slash stays orange — it's always orange)
  • On orange backgrounds: white type, white slash (unified, no tension needed — we're already inside the detonation)
Mark B
The Grid Monogram
The monogram AP inside a structured grid square. Every line of the grid is present and correct — measured, corporate, disciplined. One orange diagonal cuts corner to corner. The grid is the establishment. The stroke is the answer to it. Used as an icon, favicon, or standalone mark.

Design rationale

The grid is the corporate establishment made visual — measured, regular, every line in its place. The A is roman, upright, structured. The P is italic, in motion, already breaking. The orange diagonal doesn't just cross the grid — it uses the exact corners of the square as its anchor points, as if it measured itself against the system before cutting through it. The orange dots at each terminus are impact marks. This scales from 16px favicon to large format without losing its legibility or meaning.

Mark C
The Rule Wordmark
A single horizontal rule divides the mark. "ALIGNED" sits above it — composed, upright, in order. "Power" erupts below it — italic, orange, already past the line. The rule is the boundary. The brand is what happens when you cross it.

Design rationale

The horizontal rule is one of the oldest typographic devices — it separates, orders, maintains hierarchy. Here it separates the composed "ALIGNED" (tracked out, all caps, formal) from the italic orange "Power" (flowing, personal, already free). The rule is the line you cross. "Power" has already crossed it. This reads as a before/after in a single glance — which is exactly what the brand promises clients: you're going to cross that line too.

My recommendation

Mark B (the grid monogram) is the strongest long-term identity asset — it's the most scalable, the most original, and it carries the philosophy most completely. The grid is the system; the slash is the answer. It works at 16px favicon and at 4-foot banner scale without losing meaning.

Mark C (the rule wordmark) is the most elegant and quickest to read. It's the most practical for a coaching website where you want instant clarity. "ALIGNED" above the line, "Power" below and past it — the whole promise in two words and a rule.

Mark A (the slash wordmark) is the most subtle — the detonation is small, almost missed at first, then unmissable once you see it. That's the brand in a nutshell too.
