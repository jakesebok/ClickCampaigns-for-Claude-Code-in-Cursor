# Empathy Section — Copy Critique & Rewrite

**Reviewer:** Ryan (Direct Response Copywriter)  
**Date:** March 2025  
**Purpose:** Honest critique of current empathy cards; recommendations for stronger copy

---

**Section:** "You're successful… but at what cost?" / "Do you see any of these in your business?"

## Ryan — Line-by-Line Critique

### Section Headline: "You're successful… but at what cost?"

**Strengths:** Creates tension. The ellipsis works. "At what cost" is a classic pain-agnostic hook.

**Weaknesses:** "Successful" can feel like a compliment that doesn't land for someone who's struggling. Some visitors will think, "I'm not even successful—I'm stuck." The knowledge base says they're often *plateaued* or *operating in chaos*—they may not identify as "successful."

**Recommendation:** Consider alternatives that include the plateaued/chaos segment:
- **Option A (keep):** "You're successful… but at what cost?" — Works for the $150K–$750K revenue crowd who *look* successful.
- **Option B:** "You're building something… but at what cost?" — Broader, includes those who don't feel successful yet.
- **Option C:** "From the outside, it looks like success. But you know the cost." — Directly from the knowledge base ("checking all those boxes looked like success").

**Verdict:** Option C is strongest—it names the gap between external appearance and internal reality, which is core to the brand.

---

### Subhead: "Do you see any of these in your business?"

**Strengths:** Invitation to self-identify. "In your business" is specific.

**Weaknesses:** "Any of these" is a bit soft. Could be punchier.

**Recommendation:** 
- "Sound familiar?" — Shorter, more direct.
- "Which one hits?" — More provocative.
- "Do you recognize yourself?" — Shifts from "business" to "you," which is more personal.

**Verdict:** "Sound familiar?" or "Do you recognize yourself?" — both stronger.

---

### The Grind

**Current:** You're doing everything yourself. You tell yourself it's temporary—but it's been "temporary" for years. The business runs on you.

**Strengths:** 
- "Temporary" in quotes is excellent—it captures the self-deception.
- "The business runs on you" is a strong closer.

**Weaknesses:**
- "Doing everything yourself" is generic. The knowledge base says they have "teams of fewer than three people"—so they're not *literally* alone, but they're carrying the weight. "Everything yourself" might not resonate for someone with one part-time VA.
- Missing the *emotional* cost: burnout, exhaustion, the slow drain. Right now it's descriptive but not visceral.

**Recommendation:**
- Add a line that names the feeling: "You're exhausted. You've been exhausted for years."
- Or sharpen the opener: "You're the bottleneck. The strategist, the doer, the fixer—all of it."
- Knowledge base tie-in: "You built a team and scaled revenue—and you're still doing everything yourself."

**Proposed rewrite:**
> You're the bottleneck. Strategist, doer, fixer—all of it. You tell yourself it's temporary, but it's been "temporary" for years. The business runs on you, and you're running on fumes.

---

### The Guilt

**Current:** At work, you're thinking about your family. With your family, you're thinking about work. You're never fully anywhere.

**Strengths:**
- The parallel structure (work→family, family→work) is effective.
- "Never fully anywhere" is memorable and true.

**Weaknesses:**
- "The Guilt" as a headline is good, but the body doesn't name the *guilt* explicitly—it describes the split. The knowledge base says "the guilt of not being able to close that gap alone is loud at 2am." That's the real punch.
- "The business is bleeding into the life you built it for" (from the knowledge base) is a stronger image than "never fully anywhere"—consider weaving it in.

**Recommendation:**
- Add the 2am / guilt angle.
- Or use "bleeding" for more visceral impact.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> At work, you're thinking about your family. With your family, you're thinking about work. You're never fully anywhere—and the guilt is loud at 2am. The business is bleeding into the life you built it for.

**Alternative (tighter):**
> At work, you're thinking about your family. At home, you're thinking about work. You're never fully anywhere. And you know exactly what that's costing the people who matter most.

---

### The Loop

**Current:** Plan. Start. Stall. Repeat. You know exactly what to do. You just can't make yourself do it consistently.

**Strengths:**
- "Plan. Start. Stall. Repeat." is excellent—rhythmic, recognizable, punchy.
- "You know exactly what to do" + "can't make yourself" creates the right tension. This is the self-sabotage / alignment problem in one line.

**Weaknesses:**
- Missing the bridge to the hero's framework: "It's not a discipline problem." That reframe is in the hero subhead—repeating it here would reinforce and create continuity. But it might feel redundant.
- "Consistently" is a bit soft. Could end on something sharper.

**Recommendation:**
- The knowledge base says they "do not need more motivation. They need a clear compass." The Loop card could hint at that—it's not willpower, it's something else.
- Optional add: "It's not a discipline problem. It's an alignment problem." — Creates a thread to the hero and The Work section.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> Plan. Start. Stall. Repeat. You know exactly what to do. You just can't make yourself do it—and you've tried the discipline fixes. They didn't stick.

**Alternative (with reframe):**
> Plan. Start. Stall. Repeat. You know exactly what to do. You just can't make yourself do it consistently. It's not a willpower problem.
