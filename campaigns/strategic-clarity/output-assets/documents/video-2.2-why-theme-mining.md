### Exercise 1: Expanding Your Peak Moments

In the Pre-Work Packet, you wrote the stories. Now we're going to go deeper. For each story, you're going to fill in three additional columns:

**Who was impacted?** Not just you. Who else was in the room? Who was affected by what you did? Who changed because of that moment?

**What was the transformation?** What shifted? What went from broken to whole, from confused to clear, from stuck to free? What changed in the person, the room, the situation?

**What mattered most to me?** This is the gut check. What value or need was being honored in that moment? Was it connection? Truth? Justice? Freedom? Dignity? Protection? What was the *thing underneath the thing*?

Take your time here. This is where the gold is.

---

### Exercise 2: Theme Mining

Now we go pattern hunting.

If you're doing this with a partner or in a group—great. One person asks the questions, listens for repeats, and mirrors back what they hear. No advice. No fixing. Just listening and reflecting.

If you're doing this solo—that works too. There's an AI prompt in the worksheet specifically designed for this. You paste your Peak Moments table into it, and the AI acts as a values-aligned theme mining coach, walking you through the same process.

Either way, here are the five questions. Answer them quickly. First honest answer wins.

**1. What do these stories have in common?**

Don't overthink it. What's the obvious thread? Maybe it's always about helping someone see something they couldn't see before. Maybe it's always about creating safety. Maybe it's always about fighting for the underdog. Whatever it is—name it.

**2. What problem were you helping solve—beneath the surface problem?**

This is crucial. The surface problem might have been "they needed a business plan" or "they were struggling with their marriage" or "the team was falling apart." But underneath that? Maybe the real problem was that they'd lost trust in themselves. Maybe the real problem was that they'd been performing instead of living. Go one level deeper.

**3. What kind of person were you being at your best?**

Not what were you *doing*—who were you *being*? Were you the truth-teller? The steady presence? The person who held space? The one who saw what nobody else saw? The catalyst? Name the role you naturally step into.

**4. What do you consistently fight for, protect, or restore?**

This one is powerful. Look across all the stories. Is there a thing you keep defending? Freedom? Truth? Belonging? Dignity? Agency? Something keeps showing up. What is it?

**5. What do people thank you for that they rarely thank others for?**

What's the compliment you get that feels different? Not "great job" or "you're so smart." The one that makes you stop. The one that feels like they actually *saw* you. "You made me feel like I could be honest." "You helped me trust myself again." "You said the thing nobody else would say."

---

### The Theme Summary

After you answer those five questions, fill in the Theme Summary box:

- **Repeated words/images/needs I keep seeing**
- **The deeper human need underneath these stories is...**
- **My strongest "move from → to" pattern is...**
- **The sentence that feels most true (raw, messy) is...**

That last one—the messy one—is usually closest to your real Driving Fire. Don't clean it up. Just write it.

---

### If You're Using the Solo AI Prompt

Copy the prompt from the worksheet. Paste it into ChatGPT, Claude, or whatever you use. Then paste your Peak Moments table underneath it.

The AI will act as a values-aligned theme mining coach—warm, direct, no fluff. It'll ask you questions one at a time. It'll push you for specificity. It'll mirror back patterns. And it'll help you draft your Driving Fire by the end of the conversation.

A couple tips for the AI session:

- Answer in full sentences. Give it texture. The more you give, the better it mines.
- When it mirrors something back and it doesn't land, say so. "That doesn't feel right. It's more like..."
- Don't rush to the Driving Fire statement. Let the pattern emerge. The statement comes from the pattern, not from forcing words into a template.
